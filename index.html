    🎛️ Flex Playbook (How to Decide Each Morning)

    ▶ Weather-first decision tree

    Pick your track each day in ~60 seconds.

    • Rain waterfalls viewpoints, short forest walks, museums/cafés, or a soak. Save exposed ridgelines for a clear window.
    • Wind avoid high exposed viewpoints; prefer sheltered valleys/lakeside walks.
    • Sunny commit to a "Big Day" hike or boat/cruise. Start early for parking + calmer conditions.
    • Mixed "A-option" early; keep a "B-option" close-by that's shorter or more sheltered.
    ▶ Daily logistics checklist
    • Fuel + snacks: top up before long corridors (Fiordland/Milford especially).
    • Roads: check NZTA journeys + any DOC alerts before committing to a drive.
    • Reservations: cruises, specialty dining, and hot pools often need advance booking (or flexible cancellation).
    • Sunset/Night: keep a "golden hour" stop and a "night-sky" backup (Tekapo region is ideal).
    • Energy: if you feel cooked, swap in a lakeside stroll + early dinner rather than pushing a long drive + hike.

    ⠿ Day 1
    Wine CountryArrival ▲▼ collapse
    ⠿

    Arrive at Queenstown Airport. Collect your rental vehicle and head southeast through the dramatic Kawarau Gorge on SH6 — the road to Alexandra (1h 45m) passes right through the heart of Gibbston Valley wine country, so allow time for a cellar door stop or two en route. Check into Grey Ridge Vineyard this afternoon for your first night in Central Otago.

    ⠿
    🍽️ Dining: Lunch en route: Amisfield Winery Bistro (Lake Hayes, 15 min from airport — excellent winery lunch, "Trust the Chef" option) or grab a quick bite in Cromwell. Dinner: Optional 3-course dinner at Grey Ridge with owners Sue and Paul ($95pp, book when you reserve — highly recommended for your first night).
    ⠿
    🍷 En Route: Gibbston Valley Cellar Doors (stop on the drive to Alexandra)

    Central Otago is NZ’s world-famous Pinot Noir capital — the world’s southernmost commercial wine region with six sub-regions. Cool alpine climate and schist soils produce intensely perfumed, complex Pinots. Cycle the Gibbston River Trail between cellar doors, or drive the dramatic gorge road.

    Gibbston Valley Winery — Where it all began (first commercial vintage 1987). NZ’s largest wine cave (1,400m³ blasted into a mountain) — cave tour unmissable. Bistro restaurant, e-bike hire. 25 min from QT.
    Peregrine Winery — Cellar door shaped like a falcon’s wing in flight — an architectural award-winner. Organic, conservation-focused. Excellent Pinot Noir and Riesling. Tastings Tue–Sat, book ahead.
    Chard Farm — 2km down a precipitous gravel cliff road above the Kawarau River. Rocky ledge winery with breathtaking gorge views. The Vintner’s Pinot is a local secret — always buy a bottle.
    Kinross Cellar Door — Five boutique labels under one roof: Coal Pit, Hawkshead, Kinross, Valli (pioneer Grant Taylor’s single-terroir pinots), and Wild Irishman (founder Alan Brady’s retirement project). Most educational tasting in the valley. Vineyard cottages + hot tub + bistro dinner.
    Mt Rosa — Hidden gem in a converted woolshed. Personal, casual, perfectly relaxed. Their rosé is ideal on a warm afternoon.
    Stoneridge Estate (Lake Hayes) — Private cedar hot tubs overlooking the vineyard. Quieter, more romantic than Onsen. Book ahead.
    Amisfield Winery (Lake Hayes) — ‘Trust the Chef’ long lunch, premium estate produce, award-winning fruit-forward Pinot. Stone building with roaring fire in winter. One of NZ’s best winery lunches.

    ⠿
    🍺 Craft Beer: The Full Queenstown & Arrowtown Brewery Map

    Queenstown and Wānaka together have more breweries per capita than anywhere else in NZ — a legacy of the same adventure-driven, settle-somewhere-beautiful culture that fills the ski fields and trails.

    🏠 In Queenstown town & marina:
    Altitude Brewing — Queenstown’s original craft brewery (est. 2013), right on the marina. Ski chalet decor, 12+ taps, food trucks outside. Gold medal Mischievous Kea IPA, Sled Dog Hazy, and the rolling Jam Sessions fruited sour series. Made a Royalburn Station Italian pilsner with Nadia Lim’s barley. The best post-bike-ride pint in QT.
    Smiths Craft Beer House — 21 rotating taps of NZ’s finest, upstairs on Shotover Street with Remarkables views. Award-winning Beef Short Rib Burger. Hosts the annual NZ IPA Challenge each winter.
    Searchlight Brewery — 18 taps, independently owned, 15-min walk from town in Industrial Place. Always innovating. Catch Tacos Jaguar food truck out front.
    Perky’s Floating Bar — NZ’s only floating bar, moored on Lake Wakatipu. Central Otago wines, local craft beers, handcrafted cocktails. No kitchen — bring your own food. Panoramic mountain views. Open daily 12pm–11pm.

    🚗 Near Queenstown (5–15 min):
    Canyon Brewing (Arthurs Point) — Beer garden arguably NZ’s most scenic, front row to the Shotover River gorge. Bold, adventure-DNA brews. Kitchen that “gives a damn” with locally sourced eats. Beers also poured at Coronet Peak and The Remarkables ski fields.
    Handlebar BBQ & Brewery (Frankton, near airport) — Smoked meat platters, breakfast baps, and their own Pale Ale + Pilsner alongside guest taps. Unpretentious, great vibes. Mon–Thu 7am–3pm, Fri 7am–8pm.
    Cargo Brewing at Gantley’s (Arthurs Point) — A brewery inside a Gibbston Valley vineyard, with their taproom at Gantley’s Hotel — Queenstown’s oldest pub. Core range + rotating seasonals including a choco-coffee porter. The wine + beer crossover is fitting.

    🌿 Arrowtown (20 min from QT — the gold rush village):
    Arrowtown Brewing Co — One of NZ’s most charming brewery origin stories: a photographer, The Exponents bassist Dave Gent, and Navman founder Sir Peter Maire dreamed it up over pints at the Fork & Tap. Their Sawpit Pilsner (named after the local hike) and tropical IPA are excellent. No taproom — find them at Slow Cuts restaurant, Fork & Tap, and Bendix Stables in Arrowtown.
    Lake & Wood Brew Co — Micro-brewery in the back of Slow Cuts restaurant. Four styles on tap including a barrel-aged ale matured in Amisfield Pinot Noir barrels — arguably the purest expression of the wine-meets-beer spirit of Central Otago. Watch Jay brew and taste a golden drop at the source.

    ⠿
    🏨 Tonight: Grey Ridge Vineyard — Confirmed reservation, March 1. Choose the Vineyard Suite (up to 4 guests) or Orchard Studio (1–2 guests). Both are private self-contained units with vineyard views. Upon arrival, owners Sue and Paul will take you on a private tour of their 4-hectare estate and pour you through their four wines on the terrace. Cooked breakfast included the next morning. Optional 3-course dinner $95pp — book when you confirmed your stay, or call ahead. Walk around the estate at sunset with a glass of their Pinot Rosé in hand and you'll know exactly why Central Otago is New Zealand's most beloved wine region.
    ⠿
    🚗 Drive Notes: Queenstown → Alexandra via Kawarau Gorge
    • SH6 via Kawarau Gorge — The main route, 1h 45m. Dramatic schist gorge with the turquoise Kawarau River below. Pass the Kawarau Bridge Bungy (world's first commercial bungy, 1988 — worth a quick stop to watch).
    • Fuel up in Cromwell — last large town before Alexandra. Good petrol and supermarket.
    • Alexandra arrival tip: Stop at the Alexandra i-SITE Visitor Centre on Centennial Avenue for maps of the cycle trails and a winery overview before heading out to Grey Ridge.
    ⠿ Day 2
    CyclingWine Country ▲▼ collapse
    ⠿

    Enjoy your included cooked breakfast at Grey Ridge — Sue and Paul's breakfasts are a highlight in themselves. Check out at leisure and spend a full day exploring the Alexandra Basin. You're already in the heart of it: world-class cycling, boutique cellar doors that are literally people's homes, and the dramatic schist canyon landscape all within a short drive.

    ⠿
    🚴 Key Activity: Lake Dunstan Trail

    Start at Clyde or Cromwell depending on wind direction. One of NZ's premier rail trails — turquoise lake, cliffs, orchards. Hire from Altitude Bikes (E-Bike ~$100 | Standard ~$55).

    ⠿
    🧖 The Soak: River Rock Estate

    Intimate private hot tubs set among olive groves with Dunstan Range views. Book ahead.

    ⠿
    🍽️ Dining: Lunch: The Merchant of Clyde (Artisan deli, stone fruit). Dinner: Carrick Winery (Famous Central Otago lamb).
    ⠿
    🍷 Bannockburn Wine Trail (Felton Road — the Pinnacle)

    Bannockburn is considered the holy grail of Central Otago wine — officially recognised as its own Geographical Indication in 2022. The gold-mining history, schist rock, and intense summer heat create wines of extraordinary concentration. Felton Road alone is worth the detour from Queenstown.

    Felton Road Winery — Widely regarded as Central Otago’s greatest producer. Free vineyard tour + tasting, Mon–Fri by appointment only (call ahead). Biodynamic farming, old vines, multiple single-vineyard Pinots. The wines are as good as anything from Burgundy. Don’t miss.
    Mt Difficulty Winery — Bannockburn icon with sweeping terrace views over the Cromwell Basin. Award-winning restaurant. Roaring Meg Pinot Noir is the crowd favourite. Book the terrace for lunch.
    Te Kano Estate — Stunning light-filled architectural cellar door on Felton Road, overlooking the Kawarau River. Small batch, single-vineyard wines including unusual Cabernet Franc and Blanc de Noir. Rapidly rising reputation.
    Rabbit Ranch — Fun and unpretentious. Juicy, fruit-forward Pinot designed for immediate drinking. Perfect casual afternoon stop. Their crisp rosé is ideal on a warm Bannockburn day.
    Desert Heart — Boutique cellar door with cult following. Intimate, personal tastings with a direct connection to the winemaker’s story.
    Terra Sancta — Four vineyards around Bannockburn open for public viewing in summer (appointment other times). Signature Single Block wines from Pinot Noir.
    Two Paddocks (Alexandra) — Sam Neill’s winery. Vineyards in both Alexandra and Bannockburn (on Felton Road). Big structured Pinots with complex whole-bunch character. Cellar door in Alexandra.
    Cycle tip: Hire e-bikes from Carrick, Cromwell, or Classic Car Wine Tours to ride the Bannockburn vineyard loop. The Lake Dunstan Cycle Trail passes many cellar doors — Carrick’s pizza truck serves passing cyclists.

    ⠿
    🍷 Cromwell Basin Wineries (30 min north of Bannockburn)

    The Cromwell Basin has the most concentrated cluster of cellar doors in Central Otago — many within walking distance of each other near Lowburn and Pisa. Great if you’re short on time.

    Quartz Reef — Known for exceptional méthode traditionnelle sparkling wines as well as Pinot Noir. The Pétillant Naturel is a revelation.
    Wooing Tree Cellar Door & Kitchen — Newly renovated and one of the most welcoming in the basin. Great food pairing options.
    Misha’s Vineyard, Wild Earth, Pisa Range Estate — All within a stone’s throw of each other near the Pisa range. Each offers a distinctly different style.
    Bannockburn Hotel — Not a winery, but has one of NZ’s most extensive wine lists (200+ wines, 60+ by the glass). The perfect back-up on a rainy afternoon.

    ⠿
    🍷 Alexandra Basin: NZ's Most Personal Cellar Doors

    Alexandra is Central Otago's most southerly sub-region and its most intimate. Many cellar doors here are literally someone's home — you taste wine on their outdoor terrace, meet the person who grew every grape, and leave feeling like you've made friends rather than ticked a tourist box. The daily temperature swings (sometimes 30°C range) create vivid, aromatic wines unlike anywhere else.

    Grey Ridge Vineyard — Sue and Paul's 4-hectare boutique vineyard on Letts Gully Road. Four beautiful wines: Alchemy (White Pinot Noir, complex and age-worthy), Horizon (Pinot Rosé, dangerously easy drinking), Gravity (classic Pinot), and the Reserve. Tasting on the terrace with vineyard and mountain views. Groups of 4–14 by advance booking. Also offers B&B stay — the most special overnight in the region.
    Judge Rock Wines — 36 Hillview Road. Open 7 days, 11am–5pm. Multiple Decanter and IWSC gold medals for their Pinot Noir and St Laurent. Self-describe as “the Higgs Boson of world Pinot Noir” — almost invisible but detectible by their mass of awards. Rail Trail at the front door. Vineyard cottage also available to stay.
    Dunstan Road Wines — Tiny family artisan operation run by Marc and Sarah. Tastings in a beautiful vineyard courtyard, summer 12–6pm (book outside summer). The standout experience: tasting wine directly from the barrel before it's bottled. Their sheep roam the vineyard doing the "leaf-picking" — an entirely natural and adorable sight.
    Black Ridge Vineyard — One of NZ's original pioneering vineyards (1981), set in the spectacular schist landscape of Conroys Gully above the Alexandra Basin. Small tasting room, $10/person. A piece of Central Otago wine history.
    Monte Christo Winery — A Frenchman, Jean Désiré Féraud, first planted vines in this exact location in 1864 — making it the birthplace of Alexandra wine. The stone winery still stands in the schist landscape. One of the most historically layered stories in NZ wine.
    Also worth exploring: Three Miners Vineyard • Como Villa • Ruru Wines • Immigrant's Vineyard — all small, personal, and proudly off the tourist trail.

    🍺 Alexandra Craft Beer: Ferris Road Brewery (Ngapapa Street — the name’s a running local joke) — the only dedicated craft brewery in Central Otago wine country. Owner-brewer Sam Forsyth always has a rotating selection of guest taps from around NZ plus his own range. The perfect combination: fresh house beer + wood-fired pizza on a warm Central Otago evening. A proper local’s pub among all the cellar doors.

    ⠿
    🍷 Pisa Range & Bendigo: The Boldest Pinots in Central Otago

    Cromwell / Pisa Range additions:
    Burn Cottage Vineyard (Pisa foothills) — A 24-hectare biodynamic estate owned by the Sauvage family (who also own the celebrated Koehler Ruprecht estate in Germany's Pfalz). Closed farm system, biodynamic certification, and truly exceptional Pinot Noir. No cellar door walk-ins — buy online or find at top restaurants. A collector's wine.
    Aurum Wines (Lowburn) — Certified organic, single-vineyard. The cellar door is in the original farm cottage — intimate, rustic, personal. Luxury vineyard accommodation available.
    Domaine Thomson (Mt Pisa) — Airy tasting room with flooring made from old rail posts and artefacts from explorer John Turnbull Thomson (owner David Hall-Jones' great-great-grandfather). Take a glass out to the terrace for Lake Wānaka views and a platter of nibbles.
    Rockburn Wines — Started as a 3-hectare hobby block at Lake Hayes in 1991. Now 45+ hectares across Lake Hayes, Gibbston, and Mt Pisa. Their Cromwell cellar door has sweeping views over Lake Dunstan.

    Bendigo Sub-region (northeast of Cromwell, NZ's warmest vineyard slopes):
    Mondillo Winery — High above Cromwell Basin with panoramic views over Lake Dunstan. By-appointment only — which makes the service exceptionally attentive. Only five varietals, each made with obsessive care.
    Cloudy Bay Shed (Bendigo) — Cloudy Bay's Central Otago cellar door for their Te Wahi Pinot Noir. Spicy, complex, forest-floor character that's completely different from their famous Marlborough style.
    Maori Point Winery — Intimate Bendigo cellar door with north-facing vine views over Lake Dunstan.

    ⠿
    🏨 Second Night — Stay in Alexandra Area:
    ✨ Judge Rock Cottage (Hillview Rd, Alexandra) — Vineyard cottage sleeping 6, rail-trail-adjacent, panoramic views. Self-contained and private. Multiple gold medal Pinot Noirs available next door at their cellar door (open 7 days, 11am–5pm). The "Higgs Boson of world Pinot Noir" — their words, earned by their Decanter and IWSC trophies.
    🌿 River Rock Estate (Cromwell) — Boutique B&B with hot pools + sauna overlooking the vineyard and the Pisa Range. Luxury stand-alone ensuite rooms + two-bedroom cottage. Complimentary evening wine tasting with hosts Graeme and Jan-Marie at 5pm daily.
    Budget: Alexandra Tourist Park · Central Chalets Alexandra

    ⠿

    Drive south into the Catlins — a remote, rain-soaked stretch of coast nicknamed the "Waterfall Highway". Allow a full exploration day; roads are winding and rewarding.

    ⠿
    📍 Key Stops (north to south)
    • Nugget Point Lighthouse: The Catlins' #1 icon — 15-min walk to dramatic headland with penguins, seals, and "Nugget" rock islets below. Sunrise is unmissable.
    • Cannibal Bay: Wild beach where NZ sea lions coat themselves in sand to blend in as boulders. Keep 10m distance.
    • Purakaunui Falls: Triple-tiered cascade — one of NZ's most photographed waterfalls. 10 min walk, iconic.
    • McLean Falls: 22m drop into native bush — the tallest and most dramatic waterfall in the Catlins.
    • Matai & Horseshoe Falls: Two-for-one stop — 30-min return through mossy forest.
    • Jack's Blowhole: 55m deep sea chasm — 10-min walk from Jacks Bay.
    • Florence Hill Lookout: Best coastal viewpoint in the Catlins — sweeping ocean and rainforest panorama from a quick roadside stop.
    • Tautuku Bay: Massive, almost always empty beach with dune and rainforest backdrop. Exceptional at golden hour.
    • Yellow-eyed penguin spotting at dusk from hides near Papatowai — one of the world's rarest penguins.
    ⠿
    🧖 The Soak: LongRidge Estate

    Wood-fired private hot tubs near Invercargill/Otatara. Rustic and unhurried.

    ⠿
    🍽️ Dining: The Point Cafe (seafood chowder, Catlins staple, Papatowai) · Lumberjack Café (Owaka — reliable comfort food, great coffee, local favourite) · Niagara Falls Cafe (cosy stop near Niagara Falls village, good baked goods). Stock up on fuel in Owaka — only 24/7 self-service petrol in the Catlins.
    ⠿
    🏨 Stay: Papatowai or Owaka area — small local lodges and motels. Book ahead as options are limited.
    ⠿
    🌿 Secret Waterfall: Koropuku Falls

    Koropuku Falls — The Catlins' best-kept secret. A 10m waterfall reached via a 15-min unofficial jungle track through dense native forest. Not on any DOC map or visitor centre handout. Find it 24km from Papatowai on the Chaslands Hwy. You will almost certainly have it entirely to yourself. Rated the #1 Catlins waterfall by travellers who find it.

    🌕 Blood Moon — Total Lunar Eclipse

    Night of Day 3 · March 3–4

    This section is fixed and won't move when you reorder day cards — the eclipse falls on the evening of March 3 (your 3rd night) regardless of which activities are on Day 3.

    🌕 Total Lunar Eclipse — Blood Moon Night

    Tuesday 3 March → Wednesday 4 March 2026  ·  The only total lunar eclipse of 2026  ·  NZ has a front-row seat

    🔴 Live Event

    You couldn't have planned this better. Tonight the Catlins — one of New Zealand's darkest coastal regions — becomes your personal observatory. Zero light pollution, a vast Pacific horizon, and a Blood Moon rising over the Nugget Point lighthouse. The eclipse begins just as you're settling in for the evening and peaks at the perfect late-night hour.

    9:45 pm Moon enters Earth's penumbra — subtle dimming begins, barely visible to the naked eye. Good time to head out and get oriented. 10:27 pm Partial eclipse begins — Earth's dark umbral shadow visibly bites into the Moon's edge. The show has started. ~11:00 pm Moon starts turning distinctly red-orange as more of it enters the shadow. Colours deepen rapidly over the next hour. 12:04 am ✶ Totality begins — the entire Moon is inside Earth's shadow. Deep blood-red glow fills the sky. Stars become dramatically more visible around it. 12:34 am ✶ Maximum eclipse — the Blood Moon is at its deepest, richest red. This is the moment. Magnitude 1.14 — a strong, vivid totality lasting nearly an hour. 1:03 am ✶ Totality ends — the Moon begins to re-emerge from shadow, brightening from one edge. Totality duration: 57 minutes. 2:16 am Partial eclipse ends — Moon fully clear of the umbra, back to normal brightness.

    ✶ = prime viewing window  ·  All times NZDT (local Catlins time)

    📍 Best Viewing Spots in the Catlins Tonight

    🏆 Nugget Point Lighthouse Silhouette of the historic lighthouse against a Blood Moon over the open Pacific — one of the most dramatic possible compositions. Arrive by 10pm to scout the headland path. Torch essential. The lighthouse beam won't interfere (it faces seaward).
    🥈 Cannibal Bay Beach Wide open black-sand beach with a full 180° ocean horizon — unobstructed view of totality rising. May share with a sea lion or two. Completely flat, easy to set up a blanket or chair.
    🥉 Owaka / Papatowai Area If staying locally, any paddock, field, or elevated road will work — the Catlins countryside has no light pollution at all. Even the car park outside your accommodation will be spectacular.
    📷 Florence Hill Lookout Elevated coastal panorama you drove past today — worth a return trip at midnight. The Blood Moon framed over Tautuku Bay is a once-in-a-trip photograph.
    🎒 What to Bring Tonight: Warm layers (coastal Catlins at midnight is cold — 10–14°C in early March), a blanket or camp chair, a thermos of something hot, a red-filtered torch (preserves night vision), and a fully charged phone. No telescope or binoculars needed — the Blood Moon is stunning with the naked eye, though binoculars add texture and colour depth to the lunar surface. If you have a camera with manual mode, try ISO 800–1600, f/4–5.6, shutter 1–4 seconds during totality.

    ☁️ Cloud contingency: MetService forecasts are only reliable 48–72 hrs out. If the Catlins coast clouds over, the inland Owaka Valley often clears while the coast stays socked in — drive 10 min inland. Check MetService Southland on the evening of March 3rd. The eclipse lasts nearly 3.5 hours total — even a clearing gap at 12:30am is enough for the peak Blood Moon.

    ⠿ Day 4
    WildlifeCoastal ▲▼ collapse
    ⠿

    A second full day in the southern Catlins — the most remote and wild stretch of the coast. Curio Bay's Jurassic petrified forest, Hector's dolphins at Porpoise Bay, and the dramatically windswept Slope Point round out an extraordinary two-day Catlins stay. Tomorrow the route heads north to Dunedin.

    ⠿
    📍 Southern Catlins Morning
    • Porpoise Bay: Hector's dolphins — world's rarest dolphin — often surfing in the waves here. Completely wild, no feeding or enticement. Adjacent to Curio Bay.
    • Curio Bay Petrified Forest: 180-million-year-old fossilised forest exposed at low tide. Jurassic wood turned to stone. Also yellow-eyed penguins at dusk.
    • Slope Point: Actual southernmost point of the South Island — 20-min walk through windswept sheep paddocks. Wind-sculpted trees are iconic. More interesting than it sounds.
    • Waipapa Point Lighthouse: Wild, windswept beach with NZ sea lions often sleeping on the sand. Historic lighthouse with a red door. Tussock blowin' in the Southern Ocean gale.
    • Cathedral Caves: 30m tall sea caves accessible only at low tide — Oct to May. Check times at cathedralcaves.co.nz. One of NZ's most memorable cave walks.
    ⠿
    🍽️ Dining en Route: Pack a lunch from Owaka or stop in Invercargill. In Invercargill: Buster Crabb (roaring fire, European menu, chargrilled steaks, one of NZ's most romantic restaurants) or The Batch Café (best brunch in the city, creative seasonal menu, strong coffee following). In Bluff: Oyster Cove at Stirling Point — the only spot in NZ where you can eat world-famous Bluff oysters while looking out over Foveaux Strait. Season: March–August. Along the coast: Orepuki Beach Café (near Gemstone Beach) for coffee and food before the Te Waewae Bay drive.
    ⠿
    🚗 Bluff Detour — "Land's End" (30 min south of Invercargill)

    Stirling Point Signpost — The end of State Highway 1. Eclectic distance marker to global cities, lighthouse, chain sculpture (Māui's anchor). Classic photo.
    Bluff Hill Lookout — Drive or short walk to panoramic views of Foveaux Strait and Stewart Island. Whales visible on calm days.
    Oyster Cove Restaurant — Fresh Bluff oysters in season (March–August) with massive windows overlooking the Strait. Bluff oysters are considered the world's best — briny, creamy, grown slowly in ice-cold sub-Antarctic water.
    Bluff Maritime Museum — Shipwrecks, oystering history, Antarctic expeditions. Small but fascinating.
    Bluff Oyster & Food Festival — Held every May. 4,000 people converge. Sells out instantly — book months ahead at bluffoysterfest.co.nz.

    ⠿
    🏙️ Invercargill Stops (en route west)

    Bill Richardson Transport World — World-class vintage vehicle museum: trucks, cars, motorcycles, wearable art, cinema. Home of Burt Munro's original World's Fastest Indian bike. Surprisingly excellent.
    Queens Park — 12 acres of botanical gardens, fitness trail, golf course, rhododendron dell. Great stretch after driving.
    Oreti Beach — The only drive-on beach in NZ. 26km of hard sand — people actually drive their cars on it. Views to Stewart Island on clear days.
    Sandy Point Reserve — Coastal bush walks with easy hills and 360° views, just 8km from the city centre.
    Langlands Hotel Rooftop (360°) — Best rooftop bar in the south. City views, excellent cocktails.

    ⠿
    🌊 Western Southland Coast — The Full "Southern Scenic" Run to Te Anau

    This is one of NZ's great undiscovered drives. Follow the Southern Scenic Route (SH99) west from Invercargill — mostly empty coast, dramatic light, few tourists.

    Riverton / Aparima — NZ's oldest European settlement (1830s whaling station). Artists, fishermen, eclectic main street. Dolphins regularly visit the Jacob's River Estuary. Te Hikoi Museum tells the deep history.
    Colac Bay — Popular learner surf beach. Relaxed coastal energy. Good sunset spot.
    Cosy Nook — Tiny sheltered fishing hamlet with boats on rails, stone houses, brilliant photogenic bay. Sign warns: "Short Stay Only" (for the long drop toilet). Locals' favourite hidden gem.
    McCracken's Rest — Clifftop viewpoint above Te Waewae Bay with 180° views to Solander Island and Stewart Island. Whales sometimes spotted offshore.
    Gemstone Beach (Orepuki) — Wild west-coast beach carpeted in semi-precious stones: garnets, jasper, quartz, nephrite, occasionally pounamu. Stones shimmer best at low tide while still wet. Best finds are east of the main entrance stream (shoes will get wet). The stones began their journey in the last Ice Age, carried by glaciers from the Southern Alps.
    Monkey Island — At low tide, walk out to a rocky tidal island and climb for views across Te Waewae Bay. Free camping at the adjacent beach in summer. Named after the "monkey winch" used by settlers to haul boats ashore before roads existed.
    Tuatapere — Friendly sawmilling town on the Waiau River. Gateway to the Hump Ridge Track. Ancient 1,000-year-old tōtara tree in the Domain (35-min walk).
    Clifden Caves — Limestone cave system with glowworms. Self-guided adventure — 1.5 hrs, ladders, a pool you have to sidle around. Bring a good torch. Don't enter after heavy rain.
    Clifden Suspension Bridge (1899) — Once the longest suspension bridge in NZ. Now pedestrian-only — a lovely short walk with river views and a resident rooster.

    ⠿
    🏔️ Remote Lakes Detour — Rowallan Forest & Beyond (off the main road)

    Lake Hauroko — NZ's deepest lake (462m) in untouched Fiordland. 48km of gravel road from Tuatapere, but feels prehistoric and completely wild. Easy 40-min lake walk, or a serious 4-hr hike to the lookout above. Jet-boat tours across the lake and down the Wairaurahiri River (steepest river in NZ) offer the deepest Fiordland access outside a Great Walk.
    Lake Monowai — Remote southern lake with native forest, minimal facilities, expansive solitude. Peninsula lookout track (1 hr return).
    Hump Ridge Track — NZ's newest Great Walk. 3 days, 62km. Elevated boardwalks over alpine tarns, the Percy Burn Viaduct (world's largest surviving wooden viaduct, 36m high), wild south coast, and WWII radar station. Even day visitors can access coastal sections from Rarakau carpark.
    Stewart Island / Rakiura — 85% national park, the wildest place in NZ. Kiwi guaranteed at night, moss-draped forests, Ulva Island bird sanctuary (predator-free, kākā and saddlebacks). Ferry from Bluff (1 hr) or 20-min flight from Invercargill. Best for: full day or overnight extension.

    ⠿
    🔀 Optional West Coast Add-On

    ⠿
    🍺 Southland Craft Beer: The World’s Southernmost Breweries

    Southland’s craft beer scene is a genuine hidden gem — and it has a compelling story to tell.

    Invercargill Brewery — Founded 1999 by chemist Steve Nally. One of NZ’s earliest craft breweries and pioneer of locally-sourced ingredients. Their Pitch Black Stout was specifically designed to pair with Bluff Oysters — one of the most inspired food-beer pairings in NZ. In a market racing toward hop-bitterness, Nally went the opposite direction and created a malt-driven stout. Find it at most Invercargill pubs.
    Gadoochi Brewery (inside Tuatara Café & Bar) — The state-of-the-art gravity-feed brewery is literally a work of art installed inside the Italian pizza restaurant. Owners Chris and Nicola started by making super-dry ciders from their Roxburgh orchard (the famous “Cry Baby” and “Mad Granny”), then added a full beer lineup: Porter, IPA, Hazy, Lager, Pale Ale, Pilsner. Fresh pint from the tank with an authentic Italian pizza is about as good as Southland gets.
    Garston Hops Farm (on SH6, halfway between QT and Te Anau) — World’s southernmost commercial hop farm. The McNamee family started with 50 plants in 2016; now 800+. Their hops — Nelson Sauvin, Rakau, Cascade — grown at 46°S produce unusually sharp citrus and clean grassy notes unlike their warmer-grown counterparts. Queenstown’s Altitude Brewing was among their first clients. Worth a stop on the SH6 drive to Te Anau — distinctive trellis poles visible from the road.
    Third Island Gin (Rakiura Stewart Island) — Distilled and bottled on Stewart Island using “the world’s purest rainfall” plus locally foraged manuka, rimu, and horopito. A true taste of Rakiura. Available in Invercargill and Bluff stores.

    Double Cone Brewing (Kingston, southern end of Lake Wakatipu) — Kingston’s very first brewery, founded by passionate brewer Stephen Wood with the tagline “Pushing the Limits.” Worth a pint-stop if you’re driving between Queenstown and Te Anau via SH6 — the route passes through Kingston with its famous steam train and lakeside charm. No taproom yet, but Double Cone beers are on tap at Bendix Stables in Arrowtown and increasingly available across the region. One to watch.

    🥾 Routeburn Track — Day Hike Targets (3 levels)

    The Routeburn Track starts 25km from Glenorchy (30min on gravel). Park at Routeburn Shelter, where you cross a dramatic swing bridge over Sugarloaf Stream and begin following the crystal-clear Routeburn River. Pick your distance and turn back at any point — the whole route rewards at every stage.

    • Easy (2–3hrs return) — Bridal Veil Falls: ~8km return, almost entirely flat. Follow the river gorge through towering red beech, peer into carved rock pools from the track edge, and end at the waterfall tucked into the hillside. The gorge section is genuinely dramatic — most people walk straight past the best viewpoints. An ideal morning before an afternoon adventure. No booking required.
    • Moderate (4–5hrs return) — Routeburn Flats: 15km return. The track breaks out of the forest into an open tussock valley floor with 360° mountain walls — one of the great South Island moments. The Forge Flats swimming hole sits just before the Flats: rock pools carved by the jade-green Routeburn River, invisible from the track — worth the short detour on a hot day. No booking required to walk.
    • Full day (6–8hrs return) — Routeburn Falls Hut: 19.6km return. From the Flats, a steady climb through beech forest delivers you to the bushed edge above the waterfall with sweeping Humboldt Mountain views. The hut perches at the alpine bushline — a remarkable lunch spot. Ambitious hikers can continue to Lake Harris (turquoise alpine lake) or summit Conical Hill for views out to the Tasman Sea. Start by 7am.

    • Greenstone River — Lake Rere Loop — Ultimate Hidden Circuit: 14.2km loop from the Greenstone Shelter (1hr from Glenorchy near Kinloch). Follow the spectacular emerald-green Greenstone River through native forest and alpine meadow to remote Lake Rere. The river is extraordinarily clear — the rock formations visible through the water are extraordinary. Swim at Elfin Bay on the way back. Rated among the most beautiful day hikes near Queenstown, yet almost entirely unknown to visitors.
    • Greenstone Track (multi-day) — NZ's Best Routeburn Alternative: 3-day one-way hike along the Greenstone River from the same Greenstone Shelter carpark. Huts are first-come first-served (unlike Great Walks), remote forest birds land on your boots, and the track passes through the Tāhuna Glenorchy Dark Sky Sanctuary. The Key Summit Track (3hr return DOC walk) climbs to a wetland plateau delivering a simultaneous panorama of three valleys — Hollyford, Eglinton, and Greenstone all at once. Widely considered NZ's best return-to-start alpine viewpoint for the distance. Combine with a Te Anau detour on the same day.

If staying close to Kinloch, the Glacier Burn Track (3km away on Kinloch Rd, 4hr return) is a superb morning hike through ancient beech forest to a dramatic alpine basin with Humboldt Mountain views. Three exceptional destinations also sit within reach that most Queenstown visitors never discover: Mavora Lakes (1.5hrs south — pristine LOTR wilderness almost entirely off the tourist map), the Rob Roy Glacier Track (2.5hrs via Makarora — one of NZ's finest half-day walks), and Skippers Canyon for 4WD adventurers. Or depart via Crown Range Road through Arrowtown for the final night.

    ⠿
    🌿 Day 2 from Glenorchy: Destinations Most Tourists Never Find
    • Glacier Burn Track — Hidden Alpine Gem (25 min from Glenorchy, 3km from Kinloch): DOC-signposted on Kinloch Rd, this steep but spectacular 4hr return hike climbs 506m through ancient moss-carpeted beech forest to Glacier Basin. Above the treeline: multiple waterfalls dissolving into mist, panoramic views of the Humboldt Mountains and Richardson Range, and the Dart River glittering below. Bryant Glacier feeds the stream from a 30ha icefield above Mt Bonpland (2,343m). Almost no one does this track — you'll likely have the basin to yourself. Lightly trafficked year-round to the treeline; do not go above the treeline in winter/spring due to avalanche risk.
    • Mavora Lakes — Ultimate LOTR Wilderness (1.5hrs south): Two wild alpine lakes where Peter Jackson filmed the Fangorn Forest and the Dead Marshes. Completely free — no tour buses, no entrance fees, no crowds. A gravel road leads to a free DOC lakeside campsite that feels genuinely undiscovered. Exceptional wild trout fishing; the valley drive is stunning whether or not you walk a step. One of the great South Island secrets.
    • Rob Roy Glacier Track — NZ's Best-Kept Half-Day Walk (2.5hrs north via Makarora): Drive SH6 north through the Haast Pass corridor to Wanaka, then up the Matukituki Valley on gravel to the Rob Roy carpark. Three hours return into a tight alpine valley — waterfalls cascade from both walls, a massive hanging glacier fills the head of the valley, chamois pick across the rock faces above. Rated among NZ's top 10 walks but almost entirely missed by Queenstown tourists who don't realise Wanaka is this close. Combine with an afternoon at Rippon Vineyard on the way back.
    • Skippers Canyon — NZ's Most Dramatic Hidden Road (1hr from QT): A 4WD-only road carved into 1,000m cliff walls above the Shotover River — built by gold miners in the 1880s with extraordinary audacity. Jet boat tours, gold panning, ghost-town ruins, and a stunning historic suspension bridge. Most rental cars are legally excluded (check your policy), but guided 4WD and jet boat tours depart daily from Queenstown. Genuinely hair-raising and spectacular in equal measure.
    ⠿
    📍 Today's Drive: Wanaka → Arrowtown → Queenstown
    • Crown Range Road — NZ's highest sealed road (1,076m). Panoramic views over the Cardrona Valley and Queenstown Basin. 45 min from Wanaka. Stop at the summit viewpoint — one of the great South Island moments.
    • Arrowtown — Gold rush village: tree-lined main street, the Chinese settlement at the Arrow River, and two outstanding local breweries. Allow 1–2 hrs for lunch and a wander.
    • Arrowtown Brewing / Lake & Wood Brew Co — Find both at Slow Cuts restaurant or Fork & Tap. The Lake & Wood barrel-aged Amisfield Pinot Noir ale is a uniquely Central Otago experience.

    🧖 The Soak: Onsen Hot Pools

    Private cedar-lined hot tubs overlooking the Shotover River canyon. Book 3+ months in advance — your evening slot has likely been reserved since before you left home. If unavailable: Stoneridge Estate (Lake Hayes vineyard hot tubs) or Driftaway Hot Tubs (Frankton Arm).

    🍷 Gibbston Valley Wine Trail (25 min east — stop on the way in or tomorrow)

    You passed through Gibbston on Day 1 — now explore it properly. Cycle the Gibbston River Trail between cellar doors or drive the gorge road.

    Gibbston Valley Winery — NZ's largest wine cave (1,400m³ blasted into a mountain). Cave tour unmissable. Bistro restaurant, e-bike hire.
    Peregrine Winery — Falcon-wing architecture, organic, exceptional Pinot Noir and Riesling.
    Chard Farm — Down a cliff road above the Kawarau. Rocky ledge winery, breathtaking gorge views.
    Kinross Cellar Door — Five boutique labels including Valli and Wild Irishman. Most educational tasting in the valley.
    Mt Edward Winery — Alan Brady's second act, NZ's first Vermouth, wine on tap, BioGro organic.
    Brennan Wines — Only Zinfandel and Tempranillo in Central Otago alongside classic Pinot.
